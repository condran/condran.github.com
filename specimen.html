On the Poetics of Source Code

Why reading programs as literature reveals hidden structures of thought

14 February 2026 8 min read Technology Philosophy

There is an old idea, mostly forgotten, that source code is a form of literature. Not metaphorically — literally. Donald Knuth called it "literate programming," and he meant it as a practice: write your programs as essays, weaving explanation and execution into a single document that a human would want to read.

The idea never caught on in industry. But it haunts the margins of computer science like a ghost of a better future. When you read a well-written function, something happens that is closer to reading a poem than solving an equation. The variable names carry connotation. The structure implies argument. The whitespace breathes.

// A function that reads like a sentence
function distillMeaning(observations) {
const significant = observations
.filter(o => o.signal > o.noise)
.map(o => o.essence);

return compose(significant);
}

Listing 1 — Even in its compressed form, code can carry the cadence of natural language. The choice of distillMeaning over processData is a literary decision.

Against Opinionated Frameworks

The hidden cost of letting your tools think for you

8 January 2026 12 min read

Every framework encodes a philosophy. When Rails says "convention over configuration," it is making an argument about the relationship between the programmer and the machine — that decisions are a cost, that consistency is a value, that the individual should yield to the collective pattern.

// The framework decides for you
export default defineConfig({
routes: "file-system",
styling: "tailwind",
deployment: "edge",
opinions: "strong",
});

This is not neutral. When we adopt these tools, we are not merely choosing a technical stack — we are accepting an epistemology. The question is whether we have examined it first, or simply installed it.

Creative Writing

The Cartographer's Daughter

A story about inherited landscapes and the limits of representation

22 November 2025 Fiction

My father drew coastlines for a living. Not the real ones — the ones that appeared on maps, which is a different thing entirely. A real coastline is fractal, infinite, uncapturable. A map coastline is a decision: this is where the land ends. Every morning he would sit at his desk with a magnifying glass and a pen whose nib was finer than a human hair, and he would decide where England stopped and the sea began.

A map coastline is a decision: this is where the land ends.

I did not understand, as a child, that this was an act of profound authority. To draw a border is to assert a truth that the landscape itself refuses to confirm. The tide comes in. The tide goes out. The coastline moves. But the map stays still, and we believe the map.

Philosophy

Attention as Moral Practice

Simone Weil, iris Murdoch, and the ethics of looking carefully

3 March 2026 16 min read

Simone Weil wrote that attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer. This is a strange claim, and it is worth sitting with its strangeness before trying to explain it away. She did not mean that attention resembles prayer, or that it functions like prayer. She meant that the two are identical — that to attend fully to another person, or to a mathematical proof, or to the beauty of a landscape, is to perform the same act that the mystic performs in contemplation.

Iris Murdoch took this idea in a secular direction. For Murdoch, the moral life is not primarily about choosing and acting. It is about seeing. The quality of our attention determines the quality of our action. A mother who gradually comes to see her daughter-in-law clearly — not through the fog of jealousy and snobbery, but as she actually is — has done moral work, even though nothing outwardly has changed.

The quality of our attention determines the quality of our action.

Both thinkers suggest something radical: that the most important thing we do is not what we decide, but what we notice. The moral agent is not primarily a chooser but a witness.
